Early Cinema (1890s-1920s)
1. 1890s: Beginnings
   - 1888: Louis Le Prince's "Roundhay Garden Scene" is one of the earliest surviving motion pictures.
   - 1891: Thomas Edison and William Dickson develop the Kinetoscope, a peephole film-viewing device.
   - 1895: The Lumière brothers screen "Workers Leaving the Lumière Factory," considered the first public film screening.

2. 1900s-1920s: Silent Film Era
   - 1903: "The Great Train Robbery" by Edwin S. Porter is one of the first narrative films.
   - 1915: D.W. Griffith's "The Birth of a Nation" is released, notable for its innovative techniques and controversial content.
   - 1927: "The Jazz Singer" by Alan Crosland, the first feature-length film with synchronized dialogue, marks the end of the silent era.

The Golden Age of Hollywood (1930s-1940s)
3. 1930s: The Rise of Talkies
   - 1931: "Dracula" and "Frankenstein" establish Universal Pictures as a leader in horror films.
   - 1933: "King Kong" becomes a landmark in special effects.
   - 1939: "Gone with the Wind" and "The Wizard of Oz" are released, both becoming iconic films.

4. 1940s: War and Post-War Cinema
   - 1941: Orson Welles releases "Citizen Kane," often regarded as the greatest film ever made.
   - 1942: "Casablanca" is released, becoming a timeless classic.
   - 1946: The post-war era sees a boom in film noir, with movies like "The Big Sleep."

Modern Cinema (1950s-Present)
5. 1950s: The Rise of Television
   - 1952: "Singin' in the Rain" is released, later becoming one of the most beloved musicals.
   - 1954: Akira Kurosawa's "Seven Samurai" influences future generations of filmmakers.
   - 1955: James Dean stars in "Rebel Without a Cause," becoming a cultural icon.

6. 1960s: New Hollywood and International Influence
   - 1960: Alfred Hitchcock's "Psycho" redefines horror and thriller genres.
   - 1967: "The Graduate" and "Bonnie and Clyde" reflect changing social attitudes and the rise of New Hollywood.
   - 1968: Stanley Kubrick's "2001: A Space Odyssey" revolutionizes science fiction cinema.

7. 1970s: Blockbuster Era
   - 1972: Francis Ford Coppola's "The Godfather" is released, becoming a critical and commercial success.
   - 1975: Steven Spielberg's "Jaws" becomes the first summer blockbuster.
   - 1977: George Lucas's "Star Wars" changes the landscape of special effects and franchise filmmaking.

8. 1980s: Franchise and Blockbuster Growth
   - 1980: "The Empire Strikes Back" cements the success of the "Star Wars" franchise.
   - 1982: "E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial" by Steven Spielberg captures the hearts of audiences worldwide.
   - 1989: Disney's "The Little Mermaid" ushers in the Disney Renaissance.

9. 1990s: Digital Revolution
   - 1993: "Jurassic Park" showcases groundbreaking CGI effects.
   - 1994: Quentin Tarantino's "Pulp Fiction" becomes a landmark in independent cinema.
   - 1997: James Cameron's "Titanic" becomes the highest-grossing film of its time.

10. 2000s: The Rise of Superheroes and Franchises
    - 2001: "The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring" begins a critically acclaimed trilogy.
    - 2008: Marvel's "Iron Man" launches the Marvel Cinematic Universe.
    - 2009: "Avatar" by James Cameron sets a new benchmark for 3D technology and box office records.

11. 2010s-Present: Streaming and Diverse Voices
    - 2012: "The Avengers" brings together multiple superhero franchises in a single film.
    - 2017: "Get Out" by Jordan Peele addresses racial themes in a horror context, gaining critical acclaim.
    - 2019: "Parasite" by Bong Joon-ho wins the Academy Award for Best Picture, the first non-English language film to do so.
    - 2020s: The COVID-19 pandemic accelerates the shift towards streaming services as a primary platform for film releases.
